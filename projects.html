Project Overview

The End-Effector is a precision parallel jaw gripper designed for rover-based maintenance operations in remote environments. Unlike collection-focused grippers, this design targets dexterous manipulation tasks including payload lifting up to 5 kg, toggle switch and latch actuation, tool pick-and-place, typing, and USB insertion — applications that demand both controlled force delivery and fine positional accuracy.

The mechanism is built around a symmetrical dual four-bar linkage system, where each finger is independently actuated by a dedicated 150 kg·cm servo motor, producing pure parallel jaw motion throughout the full stroke. The gripper achieves a maximum jaw opening of 200 mm over a working range of 160 mm, with the complete assembly weighing only 850 g.

Development & Technical Challenges

The primary challenge was engineering a single gripper capable of spanning two extremes — generating enough force to lift a 5 kg payload while remaining precise enough for delicate operations such as USB insertion and switch toggling. The four-bar linkage geometry on each finger was synthesised to maintain consistent jaw parallelism across the full working range.

Material selection had to balance stiffness, weight, and surface compliance. The structural frame is fabricated from 3 mm aluminium 6061 sheet metal, with PLA fingers reinforced by 2 mm aluminium strips. Contact pads are printed in variable-infill TPU. Static FEA in SolidWorks Simulation at 400 N confirmed a maximum von Mises stress of 2.45 MPa against a 60 MPa yield strength.

Outcome

The completed end-effector demonstrates that a single lightweight gripper architecture can address the full spectrum of maintenance manipulation tasks required on a rover mission, from high-force lifting to precision connector engagement.

Project Overview

This end-effector is a dedicated rock collection gripper designed to capture specimens with a minimum diameter of 150 mm from Martian terrain. The result is a deliberately simplified two-finger arc gripper, where each curved finger sweeps inward symmetrically, driven independently by a dedicated servo motor mounted directly to a compact sheet metal housing. The arc finger geometry was chosen specifically to wrap around irregular, convex rock surfaces and maximise contact area without requiring precise object alignment. The entire finger assembly is fully 3D printed, keeping the overall weight to a minimum of 350 g.

Development & Technical Challenges

The central design challenge was achieving reliable envelopment of a 150 mm minimum rock diameter while keeping the assembly simple enough to fabricate, assemble, and replace quickly during field testing. The curved finger profile had to be geometrically tuned so the arc motion of each finger converged correctly on the target size range without the fingers clashing or losing contact at the extremes of their sweep.

Outcome

The completed gripper demonstrates that a task-specific end-effector can be significantly simpler, lighter, and faster to manufacture than a general-purpose design when the mission requirement is clearly defined. This project reinforced the value of design scope discipline — knowing when to simplify is as important an engineering skill as knowing how to add capability.

Project Overview

This project is a 2-Degree-of-Freedom (2DOF) robotic arm designed for Mars rock sample collection as part of a rover mission. The arm operates using two joint types: a prismatic joint driven by a linear actuator for vertical reach, and a revolute joint driven by a stepper motor for rotational positioning. The end-effector is a dual-finger gripper mechanism powered by servo actuators, capable of a maximum jaw opening of 260 mm.

The system was designed to be compact, lightweight, and mechanically simple, making it suitable for integration onto a rover platform operating in remote, resource-constrained environments.

Development & Technical Challenges

The development process involved translating core mechanical engineering fundamentals — including theory of machines, engineering mechanics, and kinematics — into a functional, integrated robotic system. Weight was a primary concern, as excess mass directly impacts rover mobility and power consumption. The 260 mm maximum jaw opening had to be achieved within a compact form factor, requiring an efficient gear and linkage arrangement inside the end-effector.

Outcome

The completed arm successfully demonstrates the integration of multiple actuator types into a working 2DOF robotic system. The design meets its target specifications — achieving the required reach, jaw opening, and structural integrity within the weight and cost constraints set for the mission.

Project Overview

The Deep Sampling Module is a rover-mounted soil collection system designed to extract subsurface samples from depths of up to 300 mm. The system operates in two sequential stages: Stage 1 deploys the entire module downward from the rover body to make ground contact, while Stage 2 drives the auger drill into the soil to extract the sample. The auger is entirely 3D printed using FDM technology with PLA and PETG materials. The mounting structure is fabricated from 1.6 mm aluminium 6061 sheet metal. The complete system weighs just 5 kg.

Development & Technical Challenges

Designing the module around additive manufacturing from the outset fundamentally shaped every structural and mechanical decision. FDM-printed parts offered significant weight savings, but required careful orientation planning, wall thickness control, and infill optimisation to ensure they could withstand the axial and torsional loads generated during drilling.

The two-stage telescopic deployment introduced complexity in load path management — Stage 1 had to remain rigid and stable under the reaction forces generated when Stage 2 drilled into compacted soil. Constraining total system weight to 5 kg while housing two linear actuators, a DC motor, a servo, guide rails, and a sample container pushed every component toward its minimum viable form.

Outcome

The completed module demonstrates that a capable deep-sampling system can be built within tight mass and cost constraints by committing to additive manufacturing as a primary fabrication method. The physical prototype validates the two-stage deployment concept and confirms that FDM-printed PETG and PLA components can survive the operational loads of auger-based soil drilling.

Project Overview

Ad Astra is a student-built Mars analogue rover developed to compete at IRC 2026, where the team achieved a Silver finish — competing against university teams including PhD-level participants from across the world. My primary responsibility was the mechanical design and development of the science subsystem, which included a parallel jaw maintenance end-effector, a deep soil sampling module, and a dedicated rock collection gripper. Beyond mechanical design, I actively contributed across subsystems — preparing the URDF model for software simulation, deriving forward and inverse kinematic equations for the robotic arm, and supporting the embedded team with wire harnessing, soldering, and implementing communication protocols within the master-slave architecture.

Development & Technical Challenges

Working on a multidisciplinary rover team compressed years of theoretical learning into a single development cycle. Every design decision carried real consequences — weight saved on one subsystem meant more payload budget for another, and a mechanism that failed during field testing had to be redesigned, reprinted, and retested within days.

Outcome

The team achieved a Silver finish at IRC 2026, validating the rover as a competitive, field-ready system. Every science subsystem mechanism was successfully deployed and operated during the competition. This project was the most significant engineering experience of my undergraduate career — it is where design work moved from isolated assignments into a real system with real stakes.
